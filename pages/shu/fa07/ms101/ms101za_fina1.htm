Question # 2:

            In “’Get a Life’: Fans, Poachers, Nomads,” by Henry Jenkins the term textual poaching appears. This term refers to viewers of a certain program taking its text and maneuvering it, and molding it to fit their pleasure. These “poachers” take shows such as General Hospital or E.R. and analyze them. From their analysis they make assumptions based on what they think the outside life of the character should be. In other words they bring the characters to life, but on their own terms.

            The internet has given way to a whole new means of communication. This new medium provides grounds for the viewers to discuss, collaborate, and make assumptions as to what will happen next. In some cases their assumptions go beyond the plot and take a spin of their own. General Hospital, along with just about every other soap opera gathers a following that does just that. If you search for a fan site for any soap opera, you will get thousands of hits. It is amazing to see the dedication that viewers put into their posts on the message boards. The viewers will describe intricate plots beyond the show. If there is a scandal on the show itself, posts will pop up almost immediately following the action describing the thoughts of the poster. Posters on these websites do exactly what Jenkins describes as textual poachers. They take a situation on the show and they mold it to fit their fantasy. They then come up with this elaborate plot about what will happen next. It’s as if they have seen the script for the next show. They come up with incredible fantasies, but somehow always relate it back, and back their entire story up. This, however, is how the soap opera world operates. It is as if the show that is shown on TV is simply a prompt for the online world to take it in its own direction. What’s funny though, is that some of these poachers, get caught up in the poached world. There is evidence on these fan websites that fans cannot separate the poached world from the real world. There are posts of poached text that gets poached further. In other words, someone took another person’s poaching, and took it their own way, thus poaching the already poached text.

            It is undoubtedly true that textual poaching occurs in these huge television shows. The internet provides the means for this to occur even more so than in the past. Star Trek will always take the cake in terms of textual poaching. Not many shows have their own conventions where attendees dress like the characters and re-enact scenes word for word. However, soap opera fans take part in their own sort of poaching online. Using message boards, forums, and their own websites they take every word said on the show and make their own story out of it. This may not be as interactive, but it does allow for more creativity.

            Textual poachers are as much a part of the television media as the shows themselves. They just allow for more viewer interaction, which is exactly what the studios want, because the more interaction for their show, the more viewers they’ll get.

 

 

 

Question # 3:

In some of the texts we reviewed this semester, many opinions with regard to the value and effect of mass media on our culture have been demonstrated. Particularly in Greenwald’s Outfoxed, Bradbury’s Fahrenheit 451, and Jenkins’ “’Get a Life’: Fans, Poachers, Nomads” are three examples.

            In Greenwald’s Outfoxed we see a television empire dominated by the conservative views of its higher-ups. The film shows us that the effect of a biased empire is negative. Using authority figures, most formerly employed by FOX, Outfoxed effectively demonstrates its point. It describes how one company can control what goes on the air and how that one said company can dominate the minds of its viewers, almost brainwashing them. Outfoxed criticizes the ability of FOX to control what goes on the air. It criticizes the lack of unbiased reporting that our country demands. Outfoxed does not blame the viewers, instead it blames the crooked ownership of FOX. Outfoxed does not so much offer a solution to the issue, as it does explain it to its audience.

            In the novel Fahrenheit 451 by Ray Bradbury a different sort of control is seen over the media. The control comes not from the media itself but by the government. The town issues a law that in essence requires books in the town to be burned. This is prohibiting the population’s ability to choose what information they ingest. In turn allowing the town to control what the citizens learn. The book focuses in part on the influence of books on our society and the information they contain. The book portrays the book burning indirectly as a negative solution to the problem. Though it has its own effects in the book, such a law would not be effective in our society. The more books burned the more our modern society would want to find the information elsewhere, and with the internet, this is ever more possible. The control of the government on our information has been evident since the FCC came in to play. With censorship becoming an ever more popular issue, its publication is more and more evident. With issues such as at the Super Bowl with Janet Jackson and Justin Timberlake becoming front page news not because of the event itself but because of the punishment, Fahrenheit 451 becomes increasingly correct in illustrating the government’s control over our intake of information.

            The two texts presented above show a great deal of proof of the media’s influence over out society, and the various controls that our society has over the media.  We cannot choose what comes over the air, but we can choose what we watch. If we wanted to see conservative 24/7 reports on our country we would watch FOX news. If we wanted complete control of the government over the books we read we would push for a ban of books in our society, thus implementing a book burning plan. Neither of these are desirable, but in a society as complex as ours, it is impossible to satisfy the desires of everyone, especially when business is involved.
