Left to Tell: A Personal Narrative Analyzed

        In the book Left to Tell, by Immaculee Ilibagiza, the reader reads a story of a woman who endured ultimate hardships during the Rwandan Genocide. As a result of her struggles she has gone on to write this book, and speak at many different places about her story. The text is presented as a personal narrative, a story about her time during those terrible days. Immaculee creates an identity for herself in the tone of her narrative, her style evokes a sympathetic feeling from the reader, and her she uses time setting well in order to benefit the telling of the story. Though she presents many facts, it is often hard for the reader to believe what she is saying as a result of the atrocity of the facts.

        The tone of the narrative Left to Tell is upbeat and captivating. The narrative opens, “I was born in paradise.” (Ilibagiza, 3) The reader gets an idea from the cover of the book and the full title, that this is not a true statement. It is, however, an example of Ilibagiza’s upbeat tone throughout the narrative. Paradise invokes a wonderful image in the readers mind. If we were to think of it relative to our situation here in America though, we would not find this to be true. She was born into a wealthy family, in comparison to the rest of her village. However, that still involved an eight-mile walk to school, and a house that would be considered, “extremely modest by Western standards.” (5) In spite of this, she still talks of it as though it were a mansion. During her introduction of herself to the reader, Ilibagiza develops an immediate relationship with the reader. She uses simple language, unlike other literary classics, to further develop that relationship throughout the book. Ilibagiza captivates her audience with every new scene. She uses vocabulary to simply state what she wants to convey. At one point she speaks of a man who was, “hacked to death just a few hundred yards down the road.” (66) This type of wording is common throughout the book. Ilibagiza does not side-step the issues. She comes out and bluntly states what she means to say. This aspect of the story ameliorates it greatly. It truly catches the reader’s attention. Generally, as a society, we are captivated by violence. In films, novels, and this narrative this is proven. In this narrative the language used to describe the events is simple, and therefore allows the reader to not have to think about what they are reading, but instead read with ease. It is Ilibagiza’s intention to have this effect. If she had elaborated and used fancy wording with each description, it would be impossible to keep the reader’s interest. The subject matter is captivating enough, there is no need to change it around to make it sound better, in order to try to capture the reader more. Ilibagiza’s use of a simplistic vocabulary, a seemingly positive tone, and a constant feeling of action, truly bring her story to life, and help to tell it better. She uses her first person voice to provide reliability, and trust. It is that reliability and trust that allows us to believe what she is saying, even when it may seem untrue. Many aspects of this narrative develop legitimacy between Ilibagiza and the reader which is key in a narrative of this type.

        The style of Left to Tell leads us to a sympathetic feeling as we are reading. Ilibagiza uses linguistic tactics that allow the reader to get deeply involved in the story. It seems at times as if Ilibagiza would like the reader to sympathize with her. She says later in the book, “Sorrow welled up in me, and I had to step away from the others,” (148) Her sad tone seems to beg the reader to feel for her, and have sympathy for her. It is apparent that Ilibagiza wants people to learn about what happened, but at the same time her voice throughout the narrative makes the reader very sympathetic. The picture of the front cover of the book shows a sad Immaculee Ilibagiza. This is quite contrary to the end of the book. Ilibagiza ends the book as a happy, jovial person wanting to embrace life, and live each day to the fullest. This is not what the cover shows. The cover shows a woman with a face that appears to wonder. There are dark skies behind her and leads to a negative feeling about the cover. It makes the reader believe the tone of the book will be sad. While it is sad, Ilibagiza talks about her forgiveness for the horrible things that happened. She speaks of how she has moved past the violence and looks toward a future solution to the problem. A reader would not be able to guess this by looking at the cover, instead they would see a woman with deep issues and sympathize with her. They would expect to read a book filled with sadness, and offering no sense of positivity. If this was the case, it would not have risen to the top of the New York Times Bestsellers List. Life for Ilibagiza is agreeably hard. However, she uses her experiences to draw out emotion from her reader. As a result of trying so hard to get emotion of out her readers, Ilibagiza is believed to have exaggerated her story slightly. She explains, “within a few hours approximately 2,000 men, women, and children were camped out in front of our house.” (48) This is hard for the reader to believe, as two-thousand people is a lot of people. It is not probable that that many people would fit on the front lawn of Ilibagiza. That is, not to mention that this is a gathering of Hutus, and the Tutsis are looking for them at this point. It begs the question: Wouldn’t they come running with machetes, guns, and hundreds of men to kill these two-thousand Hutus. It makes the reader think about Ilibagiza’s legitimacy. Though she tries hard to create this legitimacy, it takes a hit when she says this kind of statement. No doubt she suffered through a horrible ordeal. Nonetheless she need not exaggerate aspects of it in order to make the reader feel bad for her. Ilibagiza’s use of situations like this to evoke sympathy from the reader is unnecessary, but effective. They do work if they are believed. Granted there are many irrefutable facts presented in this novel, it is safe to say that some of the stories are embellished by Ilibagiza to emphasize her story or a part of her story. While this is not an accepted practice, it does serve its purpose. Many of the readers finish the book and think about what Ilibagiza went through, and they grieve for her and her losses. It is seemingly clear from the last few chapters that Ilibagiza does not want this, but at the same time she contradicts herself by using words and stories to bring out those emotions in her many readers.

        Time, and setting also play a large role in Ilibagiza’s narrative. Ilibagiza uses the events of the Rwandan Holocaust to tell her story. She does a splendid job at the beginning of the narrative explaining her surrounding town, and her life before the holocaust. In fact she spends a significant portion of the beginning of the book talking about life before the holocaust. Her setting is a calm one before the holocaust, but she changes that setting explicitly, when the genocide starts. When the genocide starts she clearly states to the reader that things had changed. Life was going to become more difficult, and more dangerous. Ilibagiza conveyed this well to her readers over the span of multiple paragraphs. In doing so she explains how time has changed. The genocide lasted about one-hundred days. She tries to condense before the genocide, during the genocide, and her life after the genocide into just 210 pages. It is safe say that she left events out of her written story for the sake of saving pages, and maintaining the interest of her reader. Ilibagiza tells the reader that she enjoyed writing the book. She explains her motivation for writing the book as a way to help others to forgive just as she did. Ilibagiza’s use of time and setting to further the telling of her story really served its purpose, and allows the reader to truly visualize what is happening in the narrative.

        Ultimately, Ilibagiza’s story has touched many lives. It is a result of the first-person style that she uses that this is achieved. If Ilibagiza had used third-person, it would have created a disconnected story. The reader would not have been able to get as into the story as they were able to with first-person. It was the goal of Ilibagiza to achieve this connection. The book conveys a wonderful message of forgiveness, while also educating the world about the atrocity of the Rwandan Genocide. Ilibagiza does an excellent job of portraying it as a crisis using her language, and time and setting. Immaculee Ilibagiza has written a narrative of her experience, which while invoking sympathy, allows us to empathize with her and share in her goal to achieve peace in the world. Ilibagiza’s book started out with, “I was born in paradise.” (3) It is only fitting that she end the book with a statement of her theme: “Forgiveness is all I have to offer.”
